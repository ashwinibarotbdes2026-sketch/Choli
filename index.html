Chapter 03 · Vision

A line of thought.

To reposition lingerie from a concealed, disposable underlayer to a visible, durable, and materially conscious garment — rooted in indigenous cotton systems such as organic cotton and kala cotton.

Mission

  1. Develop a category of non-synthetic, repairable intimate wear using rain-fed, low-input fibres (kala cotton) and certified organic cotton.

  2. Challenge the dominant aesthetic of invisibility in lingerie by legitimising the visibility of bra straps as a styling language.

  3. Extend garment life cycles through design-for-repair frameworks: user manuals and brand-supported mending.

  4. Embed craft practices (block printing) within contemporary intimate wear without reducing them to surface ornament.

Chapter 04

The waste arithmetic of intimate wear.

Intimate wear is, by category, a disproportionately high-waste object. The reasons are not incidental. They are designed in.

०१
Material complexity
Polyester, nylon, and elastane blends — bonded, fused, layered. Once mixed, they cannot be unmixed for recycling.
०२
Component hybridity
Wires, hooks, elastics, sliders, foams. Six material families inside a single garment. None of them separate at end-of-life.
०३
Low repairability
Elastic fatigues; stitching is fine and concealed. The garment fails as a whole even when 80% of it is intact.
०४
End-of-life barriers
Hygiene constraints on resale. Mixed fibres reject recycling streams. Every used bra is a one-way object.
०५
Landfill dependency
The result: near-total dependency on landfill — with decomposition timelines measured in decades to centuries.
Cotton ~ 1 yr Polyester ~ 200 yrs

Chapter 05

What we propose, instead.

Four moves, made together. Each one is small. Together, they re-organise the object.

०१

Mono / low-blend natural materials

Kala cotton and certified organic cotton. Single-fibre constructions wherever possible — recyclable in principle, biodegradable in fact.

०२

Simplified construction logic

Fewer components. Soft structuring through cut, ties, and layering — not wires, not foams, not engineered compression.

०३

Repairable architecture

Modular components — straps, bands, cups separable where possible. Reinforced stress points. A construction that anticipates wear.

०४

Extended use cycles

A warranty-backed brand repair service. A printed care & mend manual. Garments designed to age, not to expire.

Chapter 06

Three positions.

Pillar 01

Visibility as intent

Straps are designed to be seen, not hidden. Lingerie operates as a layered garment, not a base layer. Visibility is not exhibitionism — it is a refusal of the inherited shame around intimate construction.

"Show them straps."Garment autonomy through visibility.
Pillar 02

Anti-synthetic construction

No elastane-heavy dependency. Fabric-led support rather than engineered compression. Soft structuring through cut, ties, and layering — not wires.

A bra that holds because it is well cut, not because it is well engineered.

Pillar 03

Repairability as ethic

Modular components. Reinforced stress points. A printed manual. A repair service backed by warranty.

Repair becomes both functional and emotional — an extension of attachment, a practice of care.

Chapter 11

TOWS — turning analysis into action.

SWOT identifies. TOWS prescribes. Each quadrant is a rule we are building the company against.

Opportunities (O)
Threats (T)
Strengths (S)
SO · use strengths to maximise opportunities
  • Position as the category-defining brand for visible lingerie before competitors enter.
  • Use kala cotton + organic sourcing to build material literacy and trust.
  • Expand repair into community-led engagement — workshops, content, exhibitions.
ST · use strengths to counter threats
  • Differentiate through system (repair + material), not just aesthetic.
  • Use radical transparency to counter greenwashing.
  • Frame repair as resistance to lingerie disposability culture.
Weaknesses (W)
WO · use opportunities to overcome weaknesses
  • Reframe lack of stretch as adjustable / tie-based design innovation.
  • Educate users to normalise repair behaviour.
  • Target early adopters (urban, design-aware) instead of mass market.
WT · minimise weaknesses, avoid threats
  • Maintain controlled scale to ensure quality and fit.
  • Introduce minimal hybrid material (if needed) without diluting core claim.
  • Justify pricing via cost-per-wear + longevity narrative.

Chapter 13

Packaging that refuses to be packaging.

A modular fabric system — made from upcycled textile waste — that converts, through folds and buttons, into four utility forms. Single-use becomes long-use.

०१

Shopping bag

Loops + buttons along the edges secure all corners.

०२

Mini organiser

Tuck the handle inside; secure the corners.

०३

Tiffin bag

Place tiffin; button up the edges.

०४

Mini bag

Fold to a rectangle, align the edges.

QR · scan to transform

An illustrated manual guides the user through every fold. The packaging stops being packaging within minutes of arrival.

Why it matters

Packaging is the part of fashion that has the shortest life and the highest waste. Re-engineering it changes the whole arrival ritual — and removes a category of disposable from the customer's home.

Chapter 14

A visual world in two registers.

The brand sits between two illustration languages, used together by intention. They contradict each other — and that is precisely the work.

Layer A · Composed

Refined cultural illustration

Drawn from traditional Indian feminine illustration: poised figures, controlled linework, stylised features, a sense of stillness. The composed self.

Used for: brand mark, hero imagery, founder personas, product editorial.

Layer B · Chaotic

Doodle-archive style

An archive of instinctive marks — the kind made on a napkin, with friends. Uneven lines, exaggerated proportions, unselfconscious. The private self.

Used for: packaging interiors, repair-manual illustrations, social, the founder doodle archive.

The composed and the chaotic are not in conflict. They are both true — and the tension between them is where Choli actually lives.
